How to Sell to an Agent

Keynote outline — 9 slides with speaker notes. Each slide is grounded in the source library. This is a working scaffold to react to, not the final deck.

Slide 1 · Open

When the buyer is an algorithm, the rules of the game change

  • For 100 years we sold to a human nervous system.
  • Increasingly, the chooser is an agent.
  • Tonight: which of marketing's laws survive — and which break.
Say: Open with a live demo or screenshot of ChatGPT Instant Checkout buying a product. "A purchase just happened with no shelf, no ad, no human in the aisle."
Slide 2 · The foundation

Byron Sharp's two jobs

  • Physical availability — easy to find & buy.
  • Mental availability — easy to bring to mind: distinctive assets, emotional reach, salience.
  • Both assume a human makes the call.
Say: Sharp's laws are empirically the best we have — but they are laws of human memory and attention. Hold that thought.
Slide 3 · Consumer behaviour

Delegation is a gradient, not a switch

  • 30–45% of US consumers already use GenAI to research & compare.
  • ~Half still won't let an agent check out unsupervised — yet.
  • Delegated first where Sharp mattered least: the boring, repeat basket.
Source: Bain & Company (2025); Ekamoira (2026)
Say: The agent eats habit and replenishment first. Emotional, identity-driven categories resist longest. So the threat to brand-building is uneven.
Slide 4 · The hinge

The agent is immune to nostalgia

  • Agents weigh attribute-level data, not jingles or memory structures.
  • Mental availability doesn't vanish — it changes owner.
  • The new salience: clean structured data + verifiable trust.
Source: Bazaarvoice (2025); Sia Partners (2025)
Say: This is the keynote's hinge. If the agent carries "salience," your distinctive assets must become machine-readable attributes. The brand jingle doesn't reach the machine.
Slide 5 · Brands

You now serve two customers

  • The human who decides whether to trust & delegate to you.
  • The agent who does the in-basket comparison.
  • Sharp's laws win the first; data & trust win the second.
Say: "Dual-interface brand." Keep building human trust (that's what earns the delegation) — but rebuild your product data as if it were your flagship store.
Slide 6 · Retail & fulfilment

The new shelf is an answer surface

  • Three agent types; third-party "objective" agents threaten disintermediation most.
  • Whoever owns the answer owns the customer.
  • FCs/retailers: be the clean line item an agent can trust & check out.
Source: Bain (2025); McKinsey (2025)
Say: Retail media moves from banner to being-in-the-answer. The shelf war becomes an answer war.
Slide 7 · The rails

The plumbing is already here

  • OpenAI + Stripe: Agentic Commerce Protocol & Instant Checkout.
  • Visa Intelligent Commerce (+ Akamai), Mastercard Agent Pay, Google AP2.
  • Adyen Agentic's "Feed" = your catalogue, translated for machines.
Source: OpenAI (2025); Visa, Mastercard, Adyen (2025–26)
Say: "One line of code" to become agent-transactable. The new physical availability is API + feed + protocol, not pallet + shelf.
Slide 8 · Risk & rules

The rulebook was written for humans

  • No jurisdiction yet regulates agentic commerce specifically.
  • Liability for a bad agent purchase is unresolved.
  • Persuasion returns as an attack surface (~25% manipulation vulnerability).
Source: Center for Data Innovation (2026); National Law Review (2025)
Say: Trust is the gate on the whole market. Whoever makes agents safe to delegate to unlocks the spend.
Slide 9 · Close

Win the human's trust. Win the agent's logic.

  • Serve two customers.
  • Your feed is your flagship.
  • Build machine-legible trust.
  • Decide where to meet the agent — and where to own it.
Say: Close on the title flip: the future belongs to brands that know how to sell to an agent — not just a human. Point to howtoselltoanagent.com as the living repository.
