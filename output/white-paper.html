How to Sell to an Agent

Rethinking marketing's laws when an AI agent — not a human — makes the purchase decision

1. The foundation: Sharp's laws and the assumption underneath them

Byron Sharp's How Brands Grow reduced a century of marketing to two empirical jobs: build physical availability (be easy to find and buy, everywhere the category is bought) and mental availability (be easy to bring to mind, through distinctive assets and broad, emotional reach). Brands grow mainly by reaching light buyers and being the easy, salient choice in the moment.

Every one of those mechanisms — distinctive assets, memory structures, mental shortcuts, the "available in the moment" nudge — is engineered for a human nervous system making a fast, low-attention decision. That assumption held for a hundred years. It is now, for a growing share of transactions, false.

The central question of this paper: when the chooser is an agent, does mental availability still do any work — or does it collapse into structured-data availability, feed quality and machine-readable trust?

2. Consumer behaviour in the agent era

Delegation is already underway, and it is partial rather than total. Roughly 30–45% of US consumers already use generative AI for product research and comparison, yet about half remain cautious about letting an agent complete a purchase end-to-end.

around half of shoppers still say they are cautious about letting AI agents autonomously handle purchases from start to finish.Bain & Company, "Agentic AI poised to disrupt retail" (2025)

The pattern is a gradient, not a switch: consumers delegate research first, comparison next, and full autonomous checkout last — and they delegate it earliest in low-emotion, high-repeat categories (replenishment, commodities) and latest in identity-laden ones (luxury, fashion). Sharp's laws weaken fastest exactly where they always mattered least: the boring, habitual basket.

3. Brands: from mental availability to machine legibility

The most important behavioural claim in the emerging literature is that agents do not carry the cognitive biases that mental availability exploits.

AI agents are immune to nostalgia.Bazaarvoice, "The rise of agentic shopping" (2025)
AI agents prioritize the specific parameters of a user's prompt, requiring granular, attribute-level data rather than broad, mass-appeal marketing claims.Sia Partners, "The Era of Agentic Commerce" (2025)

If true, the mental side of Sharp's model does not disappear so much as change owner. The agent becomes the carrier of "salience," and it is swayed not by a jingle but by clean, structured, verifiable product data and trusted signals. Distinctive assets become machine-readable attributes; brand reputation becomes review corpora and structured trust signals.

Working hypothesis for the keynote: Physical availability survives almost intact (it just relocates to feeds, APIs and protocols). Mental availability bifurcates: it still matters for the human who sets the goal and trusts the brand enough to delegate to it — but it is near-worthless against the agent doing the in-basket comparison.

4. Retail & fulfilment: the new shelf is an answer surface

Bain segments the field into three agent types — third-party "objective" agents (ChatGPT, Perplexity), on-site retailer agents, and off-site retailer agents — and warns that the third-party agents pose the greatest disintermediation threat. The "shelf" moves from the aisle and the retail-media banner to the agent's answer, and whoever owns that surface owns the customer relationship.

5. The rails: payments, identity and trust

The infrastructure layer matured remarkably fast in 2025–26. OpenAI and Stripe released the open Agentic Commerce Protocol with Instant Checkout; Visa launched Intelligent Commerce (and a single "Connect" integration spanning four agent protocols, with Akamai supplying bot/behaviour intelligence); Mastercard shipped Agent Pay; Google released the Agent Payments Protocol (AP2); and Adyen launched Adyen Agentic with an "Agentic Feed" that is, in effect, how a brand's catalogue is translated for machines.

If a merchant already processes payments with Stripe, they can enable agentic payments in as little as one line of code.OpenAI, "Buy it in ChatGPT" (29 Sep 2025)

For an FC or retailer, this is the new physical availability: not "is my product on the shelf" but "is my product a clean line item an agent can discover, trust and check out in one call."

6. Regulation: the rulebook written for humans

The literal "rules of the game" are unsettled. No jurisdiction yet regulates agentic commerce specifically, and human-era frameworks create friction rather than clarity.

when an AI agent makes an unauthorised or harmful purchase, who is liable — the consumer, the AI provider, the merchant or the platform?European Business Magazine (2025)

Manipulation cuts both ways: the same agent that is immune to nostalgia can be gamed through its data inputs (one analysis cites a ~25% manipulation vulnerability rate). Persuasion, the soft core of mental availability, reappears as an attack surface to be governed rather than a lever to be pulled.

7. What winning looks like

Provisional conclusions, to be pressure-tested as the source base grows:

1. Serve two customers. Every brand now markets to a human (who decides whether to trust and delegate) and to an agent (who executes the comparison). Sharp's laws still win the first; data and trust win the second.
2. Treat your feed as your new flagship store. Structured, accurate, attribute-level product data is the agent-era equivalent of distribution and shelf presence.
3. Build machine-legible trust. Reviews, verifiable claims and protocol-level identity become the new brand equity.
4. Defend the relationship. Third-party agents threaten to disintermediate; brands and retailers must decide where to meet the agent and where to own it.
