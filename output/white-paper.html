How to Sell to an Agent

How agentic commerce changes buying behaviour, and how brands, retailers and service businesses win

Executive Summary

For a century, brands grew by following Byron Sharp's two laws: physical availability (be easy to find and buy) and mental availability (be easy to bring to mind). Both assume a human makes the choice. Agentic commerce breaks that assumption: increasingly, an AI agent researches, compares and buys on the consumer's behalf.

The shift is already measurable. ChatGPT reached roughly 900 million weekly users by early 2026; the share of consumers using generative AI to shop rose from 38% (2024) to 51% (2025); AI-driven retail referrals grew over 300% in 2025; and around two-thirds of Google searches now end without a click. McKinsey sizes the opportunity at up to $1 trillion of orchestrated US retail revenue and $3–5 trillion globally by 2030.

The argument in one line: physical availability relocates to feeds, APIs and protocols; mental availability gives way to structured-data availability, feed quality and machine-readable trust. Winners serve two customers at once — the human who decides whether to delegate, and the agent that executes the choice.

1. The Rise of Agentic Commerce

Agentic commerce is the model in which an AI agent handles the purchase journey — translating intent, weighing constraints and completing transactions within boundaries the consumer sets.

For 25 years we built catalogs for humans scrolling through filters. Now AI agents translate intent, weigh constraints, and make commitments on behalf of users.The Fold (Agentic Commerce, Inc.)

The rails arrived faster than anyone expected

Through 2025–26 the plumbing went live: OpenAI and Stripe's open Agentic Commerce Protocol with Instant Checkout; Visa Intelligent Commerce (and a single integration spanning four agent protocols, with Akamai supplying bot and behaviour intelligence); Mastercard Agent Pay; Google's Agent Payments Protocol (AP2); and Adyen Agentic, whose “Agentic Feed” is, in effect, how a brand's catalogue is translated for machines. The new physical availability is API + feed + protocol, not pallet + shelf.

Big, and disputed, numbers

Analysts disagree on size but agree on direction: McKinsey, up to $1T US and $3–5T globally by 2030; Bain, $300–500B of US agentic commerce (15–25% of e-commerce); Morgan Stanley, $190–385B; BCG, $1.3T of influenced spend; and Gartner, more than $15 trillion of B2B purchasing intermediated by agents by 2028.

2. What Is Changing Today

This is not a forecast. Adoption of ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity and Claude has compounded; AI-assisted search and AI-generated recommendations are mainstream; and shopping through AI assistants is live (ChatGPT Instant Checkout, Microsoft Copilot Checkout). Meanwhile traditional discovery erodes: zero-click behaviour dominates and publisher search traffic fell about a third in the year to November 2025. See the live dashboard for the three trend lines.

3. How Buying Behaviour Is Changing

Buying journeys evolve along a gradient: human-only → human + AI → AI-assisted → agent-driven → autonomous purchasing. Delegation is partial and trust-gated: consumers hand over research first, comparison next, and unsupervised checkout last.

Around half of shoppers still say they are cautious about letting AI agents autonomously handle purchases from start to finish.Bain & Company (2025)

Delegation arrives earliest in low-emotion, high-repeat categories (replenishment, commodities, groceries and CPG) and latest in identity-laden ones (luxury, fashion). Sharp's laws weaken fastest exactly where they always mattered least: the habitual basket.

4. How Winners Will Win

The decisive behavioural claim is that agents do not carry the cognitive biases mental availability exploits.

AI agents are immune to nostalgia.Bazaarvoice (2025)

So mental availability does not vanish — it bifurcates. It still matters for the human who sets the goal and trusts the brand enough to delegate to it; but against the agent doing the in-basket comparison it is near-worthless. The agent becomes the carrier of “salience,” swayed not by a jingle but by clean, structured, verifiable data and machine-readable trust.

Four moves

1. Serve two customers — the human who decides whether to delegate, and the agent that executes. 2. Treat your feed as your flagship store — structured, accurate, attribute-level data is the new distribution. 3. Build machine-legible trust — reviews, verifiable claims and protocol-level identity become brand equity. 4. Win Generative Engine Optimisation — be discoverable and citable inside the agent's answer.

5. Implications for Product Businesses

FMCG (e.g. P&G, Unilever)

The battle moves into the recommendation algorithm and the replenishment loop. Shelf visibility becomes feed visibility; subscription and auto-replenishment become the default basket; brand power is increasingly expressed as data power — the quality of feeds, reviews and verifiable claims an agent can read.

Durable Goods (automotive, electronics, appliances)

Comparison and configuration agents do the spec work a showroom and brochure used to win. Ecosystems and lock-in shape which options an agent surfaces, and service revenue becomes the durable margin once the hardware decision is commoditised.

6. Implications for Service Businesses

Travel

AI trip planners and dynamic packaging move the battle to agent-to-agent booking; suppliers compete on machine-readable availability, price and terms.

Insurance

AI brokers and product-comparison agents commoditise discovery; claims automation reshapes the cost base. Clarity and verifiable terms beat brand familiarity.

Banking & Wealth

Personal finance agents drive automated switching and advice; loyalty erodes when an agent continuously shops the market on the customer's behalf.

8. Risk, Regulation & Trust

Trust is the gate on the whole market, and the rulebook was written for humans. No jurisdiction yet regulates agentic commerce specifically, and existing frameworks (EU AI Act, PSD3, GDPR, the Consumer Rights Directive) overlap without resolving the central question.

When an AI agent makes an unauthorised or harmful purchase, who is liable — the consumer, the AI provider, the merchant or the platform?European Business Magazine (2025)

Manipulation cuts both ways: the agent that is immune to nostalgia can still be gamed through its data inputs (one analysis cites a ~25% manipulation vulnerability rate), and financial institutions expect agent-linked fraud to rise. Persuasion — the soft core of mental availability — reappears as an attack surface to be governed rather than a lever to be pulled. Whoever makes agents safe to delegate to unlocks the spend.

9. 30 / 60 / 90 Day Playbooks

First 30 days — Understand. Educate leadership, map buying journeys, assess exposure, surface assumptions. Deliverable: an Agentic Commerce Assessment.

Days 31–60 — Experiment. Test AI discovery, build APIs, create GEO content, explore use cases. Deliverable: pilot initiatives.

Days 61–90 — Operationalise. Define the roadmap, create governance, allocate budget, build capabilities. Deliverable: a 12-month roadmap.
